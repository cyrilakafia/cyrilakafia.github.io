Focused ultrasound is exciting to me because it sits at an unusual intersection: physics, neuroscience, engineering, and medicine all matter at the same time. The promise is not simply that ultrasound can reach deep brain targets non-invasively. The deeper question is how to make that interaction precise, measurable, and useful.

As I prepare for PhD work, I am thinking about neuromodulation less as a single stimulus and more as a control problem. The nervous system is dynamic. A useful intervention should eventually adapt to the state of the system rather than assume that the same input is always appropriate.

## Why closed-loop matters

Closed-loop systems force us to define what we are measuring and why it matters. If a stimulation protocol changes neural activity, behavior, or physiology, the next question is whether those changes are the desired ones. Feedback can help connect intervention to response, but only if the feedback signal is reliable and interpretable.

That is where my computational interests fit naturally. Models of neural state, behavior, and physiology can help decide when to stimulate, how to adjust parameters, and how to interpret heterogeneous responses across subjects or sessions.

## Questions I am carrying forward

- What neural or physiological signals are most useful for real-time feedback?
- How should stimulation parameters adapt when the target state changes?
- How can we separate meaningful neuromodulatory effects from noise, arousal, or indirect state changes?
- What does a good translational validation pipeline look like?

I do not have settled answers yet. That is part of the draw. Focused ultrasound feels like a field where careful engineering and careful neuroscience both have to be present, and I am looking forward to learning how to contribute at that boundary.
